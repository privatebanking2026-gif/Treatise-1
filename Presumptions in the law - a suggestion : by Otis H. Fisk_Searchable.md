Presumptions in the Law.  
A SUGGESTION.

This article assumes the existence of more or less familiarity on the part of the reader with the discussion that has been going on from time to time, and for a century or so, of the problems connected with presumptions in the law. It is a suggestion in the solution of those problems, and invites discussion.

It seems to the writer that there exists in our system of law a Law of Proof, which has become overshadowed by one of its subdivisions, which is called Law of Evidence. The discovering and outlining of this Law of Proof would naturally lead to the clue as to the real function and place of presumptions in the law. But the writer confesses that he unconsciously reasoned from presumptions back to the Law of Proof, instead of from a law of proof to presumptions. In other words, his reasoning with and over presumptions led to the discovery, or at least a realization and appreciation, of the existence of the Law of Proof.

A list of the treatises referred to under only the names of their authors is placed at the end of the main article. An Appendix is added, by way of a test.

In some such way as this, perhaps, though apparently not until a comparatively recent time, the word “presumptio” became a part of our legal phraseology, and is still a part of it in the original form and in that of “presumption.” Presumptions certainly existed in our law long before we adopted this Latin phraseology or terminology, but we did not have sense enough to create a word in our own language whereby to name them. This has resulted from our emphasis of the distinction in our law between law and fact, especially in jury cases, wherein we are proud of and extol ad caelum the cleavage between “court” and jury. We find a tendency to apply presumptio juris to law, and presumptio hominis to fact. Indeed, we find the English equivalents given as “presumption of law” and “presumption of fact,” which latter “is not found at all in the Roman law, nor is a leading one among the continental writers. Their contrast * * * is between presumptio juris and presumptio hominis.” The true distinction is, not between law and fact, but between law and man. Presumptio hominis is not the same as presumptio facti. “‘Homo” does not mean “factum.” The trouble is, we do not translate presumptio juris and presumptio hominis correctly. The Latin genitive in these two expressions may be translated with either of the English prepositions “of” or “by,” but the more correct one is “by,” and not “of.” The exact English equivalents are “presumption by law” and “presumption by man.”

Every presumption, whether juris or hominis, is a presumption of fact, in the sense that an inference of fact is involved in it. That is true as far as the law relating to evidence and proof is concerned, and to that law we are confining ourselves here. By every presumption, in that law, a fact is, by inference, deemed proved.

Presumptio juris means a presumption (inference) of fact made by law; presumptio hominis means a presumption (inference) of fact made by man. It happens that who the man is makes no difference—he may be embodied in the form of a judge (as distinct from the court) or in the form of a jury—because a presumption of fact made by man is nothing but the result of an inference made by human reasoning (logic). But when we come to a presumption of fact made by law we have a different problem, because it is not necessarily the result of logical inference. Thus there is something unusual in presumptio juris that requires or justifies an examination into its essence. Failure to make this examination successfully has resulted in confusion of ideas. The fact that there is so much discussion and argument about it shows that the true essence has not been perceived. If we do not know whether or not a presumption is or can be a part of the substantive law, something must be the matter with our “legal mind.” If we do not know whether presumptions are or are not part of the law of evidence, we certainly must be simple-minded in the law. And what shall we say of our professional mentality if we do not know whether or not a presumption is itself evidence? And why can we not determine whether or not presumptions can or should “conflict”?

Can’t we agree on what substantive law is? Is it not that part of the law of the land which regulates relationships?

The substantive law is the law which gives or assigns to a certain grouping of facts certain effects. The “certain grouping of facts” constitutes a relationship. The “certain effects” are the rights and duties following or flowing from the relationship.

On “legal reasoning,” see Thayer, pp. 270-276, where he says: “While legal reasoning, at bottom, is like all other reasoning, yet a thousand practical considerations come in to shape it” (p. 273); and “The peculiar character and scope of legal reasoning is determined by its purely practical aims and the necessities of its procedure and machinery” (p. 274).

If the law were always observed or obeyed, we would need nothing but pure, non-penalizing substantive law, unless it be law governing pure administration. But the law is not always observed or obeyed. So, the substantive law lays down certain penalties for its non-observance. But we also need compulsion. It is itself regulated by a part of the law of the land that is added or adjected to the substantive law, and we speak of the part of the law of the land which defines or prescribes it, as “adjective law.” The instrument through which the compulsion is exercised is a court (using that word in the comprehensive sense of “judicial tribunal,” which includes the means of executing judgments, which means are properly defined or prescribed by the adjective law). 

Whether, theoretically speaking, courts are established to administer justice or to end controversies, does not concern the matter now in hand. But we do know that, practically speaking, the only thing they always succeed in doing is the ending of controversies—if given time enough! That they do not always succeed in administering justice is no fault of the adjective law, if it is itself framed with an eye to justice, which is supposed to be the case. The law simply says courts will recognize and enforce rights and duties, if they are successfully established. Justice is often outraged, not because the courts fail to administer justice, but because parties fail to establish rights or duties.

What does “establish” rights or duties mean? It means “prove” rights or duties. Rights or duties are proved by establishing or proving the relationship from which they flow. But we may call the whole process “proving rights or duties.”

The law which deals with the proof and disproof of relationships from which rights or duties flow—or, if the short-cut is preferred, the law which deals with the proof and disproof of rights or duties—is the law of proof.14

The “law of proof” is a part of the adjective law, and not a part of the substantive law.

Theoretically, there is no limit to the number of the methods or means of accomplishing the proof or disproof of rights and duties. Logically, everything that is germane or relevant should be allowed in this process of proving or disproving. But the affairs of men, and even justice itself, are not regulated by logic alone. They are regulated by logic modified by practical expediency—by that which is reasonable (or deemed reasonable) under the circumstances. Some things logically germane or relevant are not allowed in the proof or disproof of rights or duties, being excluded by this practical expediency—that is, by legal reasoning.15

Every method or means of making proof or disproof of rights or duties is a part of the “law of proof.” Four heads of them in our law are: admissions in pleadings, admissions in court outside the pleadings, judicial notice, and evidence.16

Facts admitted by pleadings and by admissions in court outside the pleadings are deemed proved by the admitting of them by the parties.

Facts of which judicial notice is taken are deemed proved.

Evidence is that which is adduced or adducible by the parties to prove what is not already deemed proved, or to prove or further prove what is already deemed proved, or to disprove what is, or may become, deemed proved.

That is the definition which text-writers have in mind when writing their books on “The Law of Evidence.” The word should be confined to that meaning. But there is another use of the word which is so imbedded in our law-terminology that it is perhaps impossible to uproot and abandon it. It occurs in the expression “preponderance of the evidence.” The expression is incorrect. There is no such thing as a preponderance of evidence. A fact or piece of evidence in and of itself has no weight, so far as making inferences from or with it is concerned. If it had, the same piece of evidence would have the same weight under any and all circumstances. For instance, the fact that a person is of a certain age, standing by itself, unconnected with other facts, would have no more weight in one case than in another. Indeed it would have no “weight” at all. What is weighed, or, better expressed, measured, is, not the fact or piece of evidence, but the persuasive force generated by it when taken in connection with the other or surrounding circumstances of the particular case. To repeat, there is no such thing as a preponderance of the evidence. What is meant and what exists is a preponderance of the persuasive force generated by the evidence. But, lest we be accused of being too technical and of splitting hairs too finely here for practical purposes, let us waive that point here—without, however, admitting we are wrong—and ask what is conventionally meant by the word “evidence” in the expression “preponderance of the evidence.” It does not mean a preponderance produced by a

But where do presumptions come in?

Presumption is a process of reasoning by which something not already deemed proved by pleadings, or by admissions in court outside the pleadings, or by judicial notice is deemed proved. If it is made by law, it is a presumptio juris. If it is not made by law, it is a presumptio hominis. The process is one of inference. The presumptio hominis is an inference by human reasoning (logic). The presumptio juris is an inference by

What is the difference between judicial notice and presumption?

In judicial notice the fact judicially noticed is deemed proved as a matter of course. The process, if it is a process, is not one of proving something by inference from something else proved or deemed proved. It is an expedient taking-for-granted, based upon theoretical or practical certainty. The theoretical certainty is based upon the theory that the courts know such and such things because they, the courts, are a part of government and those things are acts or features or institutions of government with which courts should be familiar. The practical certainty is based upon great notoriety. A presumption is something entirely different. It is a process of proving something by inference from something else proved or deemed proved. It is expedient, but its expediency is not necessarily based upon either theoretical or practical certainty. It is not even necessarily based upon probability. In most instances it is based upon probability of the presumed fact being true. But in some instances the expediency requires or justifies the avoidance of having the real truth shown.

Matters of which courts are required by statute to take notice are not considered here as such, because they may sometimes be arbitrary and not based upon any underlying principle, except that of practical expediency. On the whole subject of judicial notice, see Thayer, pp. 277-312; Wigmore, Sections 2565-2582.

That a fact judicially noticed can be allowed to be disputed is questionable. As a matter of principle, judicial notice of a fact that is reasonably open to dispute should not be taken, and evidence to dispute or confirm a fact of which judicial notice is taken should not be admitted. Indeed, sometimes judicial notice is applied at a time when no such evidence can be offered. Only such facts as are reasonably not open to dispute should be judicially noticed.

The application or non-application of judicial notice should rest in the discretion of the court, and only abuse of that discretion should be subject to correction. If a fact is reasonably open to dispute it should be held not to be a fact that can be judicially noticed. Whether or not it is open to dispute controls the court’s discretion. If a party fails to prove a fact reasonably open to dispute, he should not be helped out of his dilemma by judicial notice. If he fails to prove a fact reasonably not open to dispute, he should not suffer for such failure—indeed, in the interest of the expedition of trials, he should not even be allowed to prove it.

Of course, where judicial notice is required by statute the court has no discretion in its application. The application in that case is mandatory.

The existence of the fact presumed by a presumption to exist is questionable in both the law and the human mind. But the law applies expediency in dealing with the situation. It says it is expedient sometimes to allow the human mind full sway in making inferences in determining facts. Sometimes, it says, it is expedient to control the processes of the human mind (logic) in making inferences in determining facts. Where we have the “full sway” of logic we have a presumptio hominis. Where we have the “control” of logic we have a presumptio juris. Where the control is conditional we have a rebuttable presumptio juris. Where the control is absolute we have an irrebuttable or conclusive presumptio juris. In the presumptio hominis truth is the goal, and logic has full sway. In the presumptio juris law has conditional or absolute sway, and its action may produce a result in accord with truth, or a result opposed to truth. In the presumptio hominis human reasoning controls the process. In the presumptio juris legal reasoning controls the process, and it may or may not act in the same way and with the same results as human reasoning. That is to say, truth may or may not result from legal reasoning.

The presumptio juris is certainly a part of the law of proof. It is one of the short-cuts across the field of the subject-matter of the law of proof. To deny that it is a part of the law of proof would be like denying that a path or short-cut across a field in the country is a part of the field.

A presumptio juris may or may not be a part of the law of evidence. Whether it is or is not depends upon whether or not the inference is drawn from something that is “evidence.” If it is, then the presumption is a part of the law of evidence, because a certain part of the evidence is being used in a certain way. But the inference may be based upon something that is not “evidence.” It may be based upon something that is in the pleadings. It may be based upon something in an admission in court outside the pleadings. It may be based upon something that is judicially noticed. It may be based upon something first brought into the case with the presumption itself.

Some rebuttable presumptiones juris are offensive, and others are defensive. Offensive presumptions are those which act in favor of the plaintiff, the actor—the attacker. Defensive presumptions are those which act in favor of the defendant, the reus—the attacked. By an offensive rebuttable presumption something is deemed proved in favor of the actor, with the result that, if the presumption is not to stand, the reus must attack it. By a defensive rebuttable presumption something is deemed proved in favor of the reus, and if the presumption is not to stand, the actor must attack it. The peculiarity of the defensive rebuttable presumption is that it may become effective before any evidence or proof is introduced by the reus, and the actor must meet it in advance of such evidence or proof, if he wishes to attack it. Presumption of innocence in a criminal case is the most marked defensive rebuttable presumption. Presumptions of honesty, or regularity of business or official acts are examples of defensive rebuttable presumptions, when honesty, or regularity of business or official acts of the reus is attacked. Examples of offensive rebuttable presumptions are the presumptions of legitimacy, majority, legal marriage and sanity of the actor, when those facts are subject to attack.

Some rebuttable presumptiones juris are permanent, while others are temporary.

Permanent presumptions are those which primarily continue throughout the case, i.e., from the beginning of the case until all the proof is in. “Case,” as here used, is not necessarily identical with “suit” or “action” or “proceeding,” but refers to the establishing of the basic relationship which a party must prove in order to prevail in his contention. Thus it embraces the establishing of counter-claims and set-offs, and even certain “defenses,” as well as ordinary suits, actions or proceedings. We are familiar with such use of the word in such expressions as a party’s “proving his case” and “making out his case.”

Permanent presumptions always and necessarily relate to a basic relationship as a whole. When applied, they are always concomitant with the “burden of establishing the case.”25 Whether or not they are determinative of that burden is a question that is interesting but appears to be of no practical significance. It is certain that the two, where they do occur, are not inseparable. This will appear later, when we mention what is called a prima facie case (at page 24).

An instance of permanent presumptions is that of honesty or regularity of business or official acts when those facts, or either of them, are or is the real ultimate issue in the case. These examples are really presumptions of innocence in civil cases. The best example of permanent presumptions is the presumption of innocence in criminal cases. Presumption of sanity on a hearing for insanity is another example.

A permanent presumption always starts the “going forward with evidence (proof)” in “establishing the case.” It is thereafter that temporary presumptions arise or are applied in the case.

A temporary presumption may relate to a basic relationship as a whole, or to an incident of a basic relationship. This incident may be an ultimate fact or a probative (or evidentiary) fact. When applied, a temporary presumption always determines the burden or duty of “going forward with evidence (proof)” in the sense of continuing it in the same direction or shifting it.26 If that burden or duty is not met, the temporary presumption becomes, in a sense, permanent, but not in the sense of a “permanent presumption” as mentioned above. Let us say it becomes “fixed.” The point is, that the temporary presumption is primarily temporary. For instance, the presumption of innocence in a criminal case, and the presumptions of honesty, of regularity of business and official acts of the defendant or reus in certain civil cases. But defensive presumptions are not always permanent presumptions. For instance, a presumption in some cases that the marriage of the defendant or reus was legally contracted.

Conclusive presumptions are terminative of the question as to the existence of an ultimate fact or of a probative (or evidentiary) fact. They may thus be terminative of the case, or of a defense, or of an issue. They are either offensive or defensive. They are neither permanent nor temporary, as those terms are used above, because they do not primarily continue throughout the case, nor do they determine the burden or duty of "going forward with evidence (proof)." They are terminative, not determinative. The terms "permanent" and "temporary" are, therefore, confined to rebuttable presumptions.

This analysis seems to assist us in solving the problems about presumptions which have worried the bench and bar and writers, and have led some of them far afield into psychic refinements. The subject is a concrete one, and there seems to be no necessity for throwing up the present, age-long phraseology or terminology, and substituting another, that may itself become confused and confusing in a generation. Let us remain practical, and not go astray in fields of metaphysics, psychology or psychic refinements. We can remain practical and be analytical too. There is enough psychic confusion in the law already.

Let us look into this solution of problems a little.

It is stated by some authorities that many presumptions are not a part of the law of evidence, but are a part of the substantive law. The first part of the statement may or may not be correct. Can the last part be correct?

The analysis also applies to any sort of judicial proceeding, whether a jury case, an equity case, a litigious proceeding, an ex parte proceeding, an action in personam, an action in rem, or what not.

One thing is certain: Presumptions cannot be part of the substantive law, if by them something is deemed proved, because such a process is part of the law of proof, which, as already indicated, cannot be a part of the substantive law.

As already stated, what the substantive law really does, is to give or assign to a certain grouping of facts certain effects. To repeat, the "certain grouping of facts" constitutes a relationship, and the "certain effects" are the rights and duties following or flowing from the relationship.

The substantive law is entirely passive. It does not enforce itself. It does not regulate or control in the sense of enforcing. It simply prescribes. It lays down rules, but does not see that they are enforced by external or objective compulsion. A perfectly working system of substantive law without any enforcement by external or objective compulsion is imaginable. It could exist if the substantive law were always obeyed or followed. But, as already mentioned, it is not; so we have compulsion, which is embodied in the adjective law, a field of law distinct from substantive law.

The situation is as follows: The substantive law provides that a certain grouping of facts has certain effects, and a failure to follow or observe these effects entails a penalty. The adjective law says: "If you show that that certain grouping of facts exists, we will see that those certain effects result, or inflict whatever penalty the substantive law says shall be inflicted for failure to follow or observe those effects."

When a certain right or duty is asserted or claimed and must be proved, what really must be proved is the relationship out of which the right or duty arises, i.e., the grouping of facts to which the right or duty is attached. Isolated facts, standing by themselves, outside any relationship to each other, are of no consequence in the law. They must be grouped in a certain way before the law attaches any significance to them. Thus, before the substantive law can be enforced, certain facts grouped in a certain way must be proved.

The ultimate thing to be proved or established is, therefore, the relationship. That is established by proving a certain grouping of certain facts. All law translatable into terms of the relationship is part of the substantive law. All law translatable into terms of that proof is part of the adjective law.

The question whether a certain so-called presumption is a part of the substantive law or a part of the adjective law depends, therefore, upon whether it is translatable into terms of a relationship or into terms of the proof of a relationship.

Let us keep clearly before us that what is to be established is quite distinct from the means or methods of establishing it. A relationship is something quite apart from the means or methods of proving it. The definition of a relationship is couched in terms of certain facts grouped in a certain way. Those facts grouped in that way are the facts that are determinative of a relationship, and are the ultimate facts to be used as proof of the relationship, and also of the rights or duties growing out of the relationship, because a right or duty is simply something that the law says results from the relationship. These ultimate facts are the constituent facts of the relationship.

Thus the problem of whether a certain “presumption” is a part of the substantive law or a part of the adjective law is primarily the problem of determining the ultimate, constituent facts, the existence of which establish the relationship involved, and thus give rise to the right or duty involved. Those facts are parts of the definition of the relationship, and thus parts of the substantive law. All processes and means involved in proving those ultimate, constituent facts are parts of the adjective law.

The trouble is, it is not always easy to see what are and what are not such ultimate, constituent facts. But they exist; and it must be accepted as true that all parts of the law picking them out and assigning to them particular effects are part of the substantive law. Whatever relates to the proof of them must ex vi termini be part of the law of proof, and thus a part of the adjective law.

The relationship to be established in criminal law is one producing or resulting in guilt. Guilt is the condition of being exposed to a penalty. But one may be guilty of one or more of several crimes. Therefore, the substantive criminal law must define each crime, i.e., pick out certain facts grouped in certain ways and assign to them certain effects. Those facts grouped in one of those ways are the ultimate, constituent facts to be proved before guilt is established.

APPENDIX

“Conflict of Presumptions”
A TEST

The position we take as to so-called conflicting presumptions* seems to stand the test when applied to cases involving the question.** Let us look at some of the most typical of them.

England got the start of us in the problem. We stumblingly followed her. It has been an instance of the blind leading the blind and both falling into the ditch.

In the ubiquitous case of The King v. The Inhabitants of Twyning, 2 Barn. & Ald., 386, which was decided in 1819, and is the usual starting point in the discussion of “conflicting presumptions,” a man, after living with his wife a few months, enlisted, about 1812, as a soldier, went abroad on foreign service, and was not heard of thereafter; in a little more than twelve months after he left, his wife married a second time, and two children were born of the second marriage. The woman and the children became paupers. The proceeding was not a criminal one, but a civil action, to determine whether or not Twyning was to support the woman and the children. She was not accused of bigamy. No one was on trial for the commission of a crime. There arose in the case no presumption of innocence in a criminal case. Whether or not any one committed a crime was really foreign to the question. The question was whether or not the second marriage was legal. That question was determinable regardless of the fact whether or not a crime was committed, and of the fact whether or not a crime was intended. Two presumptions arose in the case. One was that the second marriage was legal; the other, that the life of the first husband continued until the time of the second marriage (or, expressed differently, that the first marriage continued until the time of the second marriage). They were temporary presumptions.

The two presumptions arose in sequence. They did not “conflict,” because, if they had “conflicted,” they would or could have produced

For our present purpose it is not important to know which of the two presumptions arose first, because they were both “overcome.”

The basis of the presumption of the legality of the second marriage was the evidence or proof as to the second marriage itself. The basis of the presumption of the continuance of life was the evidence or proof as to the interval of less than seven years between the disappearance of the first husband and the second marriage. These two bases “conflicted” and thereby reciprocally turned the two presumptions into presumptiones hominis—the basis of each presumption turned the other presumption into a presumptio hominis. There was additional “conflicting” evidence or proof which assisted in turning the presumption of the continuance of life into a presumptio hominis, namely, that the first husband, when he disappeared, enlisted as a soldier in the foreign service.

The case resulted correctly, because no reasonable man (homo) would, under the circumstances of the case, have found that the life of the first husband continued until the time of the second marriage. A strong point in making the inference that the first husband had died before the time of the second marriage would have been the fact that the first husband, when he disappeared, had enlisted as a soldier and gone abroad on foreign service. That meant more in 1812 than it would now. Other things weighing in making the inference of the death of the first husband would have been the facts, that finding the first husband was alive at the time of the second marriage would have made the woman a bigamist and the children bastards and all of them social outcasts. In making the inference of the death of the first husband man would pay more attention to the social and legal (other than criminal) consequences (to the woman and the children) of an illegal marriage than to whether or not the illegal marriage would be a crime. Moreover, the first husband dropped out of the case without leaving any question as to his property or his heirs. There was nothing in the case justifying a finding that the second marriage was illegal.

The final result in the case was satisfactory, but the court did not handle the case properly. There was no “conflict of presumptions.” The court was certainly wrong in turning the presumption of the continuance of life into a parallel presumption; which the court did, by saying the presumption of law was that the first husband was dead at the time of the second marriage, thus paralleling the presumption of the legality of the second marriage. If the court had said that there was a presumptio hominis of such death, it would have stated the matter correctly. There is not even a presumptio juris that a person absent seven years was dead at any particular time during the seven years of his absence.

That ruling was made in the face of the claim that the evidence showed the absent spouse was alive within five years (which was then the period of time in a statutory presumption of death from absence). The presumption of innocence in a criminal case was not involved, and the case is open to the same treatment, remarks and criticism as we have applied to the Twyning case. The absent spouse in it was also a soldier.

In Dixon v. The People, 18 Mich., 84 (decided in 1869), which was a prosecution for murder, the question was whether or not a woman, who, it was claimed by the defense and the woman herself, was the wife of the defendant, was a competent witness against him. The prosecution claimed (and introduced evidence to show) that she was not legally the wife of the defendant, because she had a husband living at the time she married the defendant, and she was, therefore, a competent witness. The Supreme Court of Michigan ruled, that, the proof having shown a bona fide marriage with the defendant, no evidence would be allowed to contradict the presumption of the validity of the marriage, where the question is of the collateral, preliminary kind involved in this case. That is carrying expediency pretty far, and the court ends up by saying: “There is much difficulty in questions of this kind. It is not safe to lay down any universal rule. Every

case must depend, in a great measure, upon its own peculiar facts and circumstances; and our decision must be understood with special reference to the facts of this case.” The question could have been decided, with the same result, under the theory that the presumption of the continuance of life was turned into a presumptio hominis. Indeed, the court argues away evidence combatting the legality of the second marriage exactly as it might, under the circumstances of the case, be argued away in a presumptio hominis. It would be much easier to argue them away in the preliminary question of qualifying the woman as a witness under the circumstances of the case—considering, especially, that the defendant is taken by surprise—than it would be to argue them away if the legality of the second marriage had been directly in issue. The Supreme Court of Michigan recognizes this. The real quarrel between it and the trial court was in the manner of arguing away the conflicting evidence. The baneful influence of the Twyning case is seen in the opinion of the Supreme Court. The court is trying to fit a conclusive presumption into that case.

A similar situation developed in an earlier Massachusetts case, namely, Kelly v. Drew, 94 Mass. (12 Allen), 107, which was decided in 1866. The influence of the Twyning case and kindred cases is seen in this case also. The question to be decided was whether a woman was disqualified from testifying against a man who was a defendant in a replevin suit, because she was his wife. She had married before, and the husband of that marriage had not been heard from for about four years when she married the defendant in the replevin suit. In deciding such a preliminary question it was not necessary to eliminate from the case entirely the presumptio juris of the continuance of the life of the first husband, as the court seems to have done, although it apparently argued that presumption from the standpoint of a presumptio hominis—when it, for example, said “as no sufficient evidence was offered that at the time of marrying him [the defendant] she had a former husband, she was rightly excluded from testifying.”

In Lapsley v. Grierson 1 H. L. This husband left for America in 1801 and was lost on a voyage in 1804 or 1805. The illicit relationship began in 1803. It was claimed that the illicit relationship became a legal marital relationship upon the death of the said husband, which was before any of the children involved were born. At best there were only two presumptions involved, namely, that of the continuance of the life of the husband, and that of the continuance of the illicit relationship. The presumption of death from absence could not arise, because the necessary period of time had not elapsed. If

A second marriage was not proved. The presumption was that the relationship between the man and the woman, illicit in its origin, continued to be illicit. It is true, as the headnote in Lapsicy v. If by the rest of the first paragraph of the headnote—“In every instance the circumstances of the case must be considered”—it was meant to say that in every case no presumptiones juris, but only presumptiones hominis, involving the two points mentioned can arise, then we say such is not good law, and we find no justification for it in the case itself. The most that can be said is, that, in the case, the two presumptions of the continuance of life and of the illicit relationship were “overcome,” but not “rebutted.” If they were “overcome,” then, of course, they became presumptiones hominis, to be determined under all the circumstances of the case. Every case depends upon the facts, but the facts in Lapsicy v. Grierson gave rise to two parallel rebuttable temporary presumptions, which were not “rebutted.” The presumption of innocence of an illegal act did not arise in the case, because the act involved, namely, a second marriage, was itself wanting. If there was a permanent presumption in the case, it was that so and so owned the property. Legitimacy was only a link in the chain of proof in that broad issue, to which link as such the permanent presumption did not apply. So that the two temporary presumptions mentioned were permissible.

In Murray v. The court said the jury should pass upon the question in view of all the evidence or proof in the case. Yet it seems to emphasize the importance of the presumption of innocence unduly, the case not being a criminal one and that presumption being only a temporary one. This is the language of the court (p. 25): “It may be true that innocence is to be presumed,

The real situation was, that the two temporary presumptions of the continuance of life and of the validity of the second marriage were “overcome” by at least their “conflicting” bases, and in making their presumptiones hominis on the questions the jury should take into consideration all the evidence or proof in the case.

The case is one of the failures to appreciate the functions of temporary presumptions and “conflicting” evidence or proof.

In Johnson v. Johnson, 114 Ill, 611 (a divorce proceeding, decided in 1885), the Supreme Court of Illinois comes very near to the proper solution of a so-called conflict of presumptions, but it cannot bring itself away from the idea that the presumption of the legality of a second marriage is sometimes conclusive. It needed just a little more light on the nature and function of “conflicting” evidence or proof. In other words, the notion of the turning of a presumptio juris into a presumptio hominis by the bare basis of another presumptio juris was at that time not well developed. “Prevailing” when applied to a presumption is still used in the sense of making the presumption conclusive.

This is what the court says in Johnson v. Johnson: “These presumptions of innocence, and of the validity of the marriage, conflict

It may, however, be safely said, that where there are no circumstances to aid the presumption of the continuance of life, the presumption of innocence and of the validity of the second marriage should, in general, prevail” (pp. 620, 621).

“Conflicting” evidence or proof does not have to be something in addition to the basis of a presumption. Such basis of itself may be “conflicting” evidence or proof. Additional “conflicting evidence or proof” adds to and fortifies that of such basis.

In the class of cases here under discussion we can see no justification for ever turning the presumption of the legality of the second marriage into a conclusive presumption, thus depriving a party of whatever benefit there may be in the presumption of the continuance of life, or of a presumptio hominis to which that presumption has given way. It might as well be argued that the permanent presumption of innocence in a case, whether criminal or civil, is sometimes conclusive when compared with a temporary presumption, thus depriving a party of the right to have the jury or the judge, as the case may be, consider the benefit of the temporary presumption and pass upon the presumptio hominis to which the temporary presumption gives way when “overcome.”

The absence of “conflicting evidence or proof” in addition to that of a basis of a presumption should not be allowed to make another presumption conclusive, because a party is entitled to the protection of the benefits mentioned just above.

The mischief done by Johnson v. Johnson, 114 Ill. 611, is manifest in Stein v. Stein, 66 Ill. App. 526 (decided in 1896), in which it was held, as recited in the second paragraph of the syllabus: “Where it was shown in the defense to a suit for separate maintenance that the wife had a husband living, in the absence of proof that he was living at the time of her marriage to the defendant, the presumption of the wife’s innocence will prevail over the presumption of the continued existence of the former husband.” The first marriage was on May 6, 1883. The husband deserted the wife nine months later, and she did not hear from him thereafter. She remarried on April 2, 1887.

And that in the face of the fact that the woman sued her first husband for divorce March 25, 1887, and she secured that divorce on June 2, 1887, the second marriage having taken place on April 2, 1887. There was a child born of the second marriage. There was a sequence of the two temporary presumptions of “innocence” (really, of the legality of the second marriage) and continuance of life. At the least their “conflicting” bases turned them into presumptiones hominis. There was no justification for making the presumption of “innocence” conclusive.

There should be no difficulty in handling a criminal case of bigamy or adultery involving the presumption of the continuance of life or, expressed differently, the presumption of the continuance of a prior marriage. The permanent presumption of innocence in a criminal case is, of course, applied. The temporary presumption of the continuance of life should also be applied. In that way the party in whose favor the temporary presumption runs is not deprived of the benefit to which he is entitled from the fact that the law makes the inference in his favor. To hold that the temporary presumption cannot be applied would deprive the party of that benefit, for, while, under such a holding, the jury ought to be told that they may infer that the life continued or that it did not continue, yet they ought not to be told anything about the law’s making such an inference. The jury should not be allowed to get such an impression, because it should consider the fact that the law makes an inference in only cases in which the law actually makes it. The jury would have to be told, in the case wherein the temporary presumption is not applied, that the law makes no presumption at all on the question of the continuance of life.

In a criminal bigamy or adultery case like the one under discussion the only practical difference between allowing and not allowing the temporary presumption of the continuance of life to be applied, lies in the protection or non-protection of the benefit just mentioned, because in every such case the presumption of the continuance of life is “overcome” by at least the proof of the second marriage. That is to say, the question always becomes one of a presumptio hominis.

On the theoretical side, not allowing the temporary presumption to be applied would be illogical, because the continuance of the prior marriage is only a link in the chain of proof, to which link as such the permanent presumption of innocence does not apply. In such a situation the temporary presumption should be allowed to function.

A jury would not have been told, that, if they found so and so to be facts, they must find there was a common-law marriage. They would have been told, that, if they found so and so to be facts, they could or might find there was a common-law marriage. No presumption—presumptio juris—of a common-law marriage would be involved in that.*

In Caujolle v. Ferrie 23 N. Y., 90 (decided in 1861), a certain man made application for letters of administration of the estate of a deceased woman, who was admittedly his mother, but it was contended he was her illegitimate son, and therefore not entitled to the letters. If he was her legitimate son, he was entitled to them. The Surrogate granted him the letters, and this action was the subject of review in the Court of Appeals of New York. There was not a “conflict of presumptions” in the case. There was a sequence of presumptions. The first presumption was that the applicant was legitimate, because the deceased was concededly his mother. That threw the burden of rebutting that presumption upon the contestants. The “conflicting” evidence or proof on that question resulted in the inference of a presumptio hominis that the relationship between the parents of the applicant was illicit in its commencement. That gave rise to the second presumption, namely, that that relationship so continued up to the time of the birth of the applicant. That threw upon the applicant the burden of rebutting that presumption. The ultimate question in the case was whether or not the “conflicting” evidence or proof on that question “rebutted” this second presumption. On this ultimate question the court divided, the majority holding that the illicit relationship terminated in a legal marriage before the birth of the applicant, and he was therefore born in lawful wedlock, while the minority held the illicit relationship continued unbroken. In short, the court split on the final presumptio hominis in the case.

The distinction between “marrying” and the marriage relationship or status is not observed.

One may not agree with the final judgments in these three New York cases, but the processes and steps of procedure and reasoning therein are not dependent upon those judgments.

The presumption claimed or contended for in the case is not recognized in the law as a permissible one. The claim was an attempt to sidestep the recognized presumption of death from absence, under the cloak of a new “presumption of innocence.” Even if such a new presumption were created and recognized, it should be based upon

only facts proved by formal evidence or proof, or by admissions by the parties, just as is the case with the recognized presumption of death from absence. Those facts would be that the absent spouse had not been heard from for seven years (or some other period of time) by the other spouse. In the absence of such facts so proved the presumption should not be applied. In our opinion no such presumption should be recognized, and the court should have so ruled. But in any case there would be no weighing of presumptions, because if a presumption is not “overcome” it becomes unrebutted, and, in that sense, conclusive or absolute; and if it is “overcome” it ceases to function as a presumption—there is nothing left to weigh. What is weighed is the evidence or proof in the light of all the circumstances of the case, including the legal, moral, social, pecuniary or other aspects involved. The inference drawn from the evidence or proof is that of presumptio hominis.

In Palmer v. Palmer there is still manifest the confusion seen in the three New York cases just previously discussed. The court still fails to recognize the true function of “conflicting” evidence or proof, and still confuses presumptiones hominis and presumptiones juris. The Twyning case is the root source of the confusion.

In Gorden v. Gorden, 283 Ill., 182 (decided in 1918) a man filed a petition to have the probate of a will set aside and to have letters testamentary revoked. His right depended upon his being the legitimate son of the testator. The burden of proving his legitimacy was upon him. It was not disputed that he was the son of the testator, but it was contended that he was not his legitimate son. The argument started with the presumption that the petitioner was legitimate, said presumption arising from the fact that he was the testator’s son. “Conflicting” evidence or proof meeting this presumption showed that the relations between the testator and petitioner’s mother were meretricious in the beginning. That gave rise to the second presumption, namely, that those relations continued to be of that nature, unless and until there be proof of a lawful relation. The “conflicting” evidence or proof meeting that presumption did not satisfy the court that such a lawful relation was proved. Thus there was a sequence of presumptions in the case. It was apparently argued that there was a parallel “presumption of marriage” in favor of the petitioner, arising from proof of “cohabitation and repute.” The court seems to have entertained this “presumption,” and to say that it was “rebutted,” by proof that the cohabitation was meretricious in its inception. It apparently was impelled to take this unnecessary step because of its desire to show that the “presumption of marriage” from cohabitation and repute was not conclusive, but merely rebuttable. Its own language shows the step was unnecessary, because it says: “Where there is clear proof of a fact, no presumptions can be indulged except

<br>

### --- Page 0081 ---

such as arise upon the proof.” The illicit commencement of the cohabitation was such a clearly proved fact. “Marriage” was not. Therefore, no “presumption of marriage” from cohabitation and repute could be indulged. The court lost itself in the confusion caused by not following the sequence of presumptions carefully and by not distinguishing between the inference of “marrying” from cohabitation and repute, and the presumptions of validity, etc., that arise from such inferred “marrying.” In effect the court said, that, from the evidence or proof in the case no inference of a “marrying” could be made, because the relationship was meretricious in its commencement, and because the presumption of the continuance of such relationship was not “rebutted.”

No marriage being proved, a presumption as to its validity could not arise or be entertained. Discussion of such a presumption would be foreign to the case.

The court was right in saying, that, finally, “the question in this case now is not one upon presumptions but upon all the facts proved in the case” (p. 194), but only in the sense that all the presumptions mentioned were turned into presumptiones hominis by “conflicting” evidence or proof. Under such circumstances the presumptions are still entitled to some consideration, as we have so often tried to emphasize.

In Cartwright v. McGown, 121 Ill. 388 (decided in 1887), L was lawfully married in Kentucky in 1841; he abandoned his wife after about a year; he went to Illinois, and there went through the statutory requirements of a marriage with another woman in 1843; the wife of the Kentucky marriage secured a divorce from him in 1846. The proceeding was one in partition, and the case turned on the question of whether or not the second marital relationship became that of a common-law marriage after the divorce of 1846. The question of the presumption of the continuance of life did not arise in the case. There was a sequence of presumptions in it. The first was that of the legality of the second marriage in 1843. It was “reputed” by proof of the prior marriage in 1841 and that the wife by that marriage was still living and undivorced at the time of the alleged marriage in 1843. Then arose the presumption of the continuance of the unlawful relationship entered upon in 1843, and the question was whether or not that presumption was “rebutted.” The court does not state the question correctly when it says (p. 397): “This formal marriage [of 1843] being void, do the facts and circumstances proved create the presumption of a lawful marriage * * * after the divorce in 1846? No record of any subsequent marriage has been produced, nor has any witness testified directly as to any such marriage; but it is strenuously insisted that the evidence will justify the court in presuming a common law marriage of the parties after the impediment to their legal union was removed.” The ques-

The Supreme Court of Indiana is so strongly in favor of the continuance of a valid marriage that it rules out evidence against it short of actual proof of a divorce or of death of one of the parties. It says: “When the marriage relation is once entered into, it is presumed to continue until the death of one of the contracting parties, and this presumption can only be overcome by affirmative proof that the marriage contract has been lawfully dissolved. Presumptions cannot, therefore, be indulged against the continuance of a marriage contract.” It ruled, that evidence that a husband, after his marriage, in a remote place for a long time cohabited with another woman, and had children by her, and that during all that time she was recognized as his wife, had no tendency to prove a dissolution of the original marriage, and was not admissible for that purpose. The case referred to is Wiseman v. Wiseman, 89 Ind., 479, which was a partition proceeding, decided in 1883. That is a swing to the opposite extreme from the way in which some courts protect a second marriage. The evidence should have been admitted for what it was worth in a presumptio hominis.

The evidence or proof is discussed as if a presumptio hominis or presumptiones hominis were involved. The importance of a presumptio juris is sought to be fixed as it exists in the law generally, rather than in the particular facts of the case because of the circumstances, including the social, moral and pecuniary aspects, involved in the case. There is a constant but unconscious tendency to decide the cases as they would be decided without making a particular presumptio juris conclusive, or as if each of the so-called conflicting presumptiones juris were turned into a presumptio hominis. The confusion is due to some or all of at least five things: (a) a lack of appreciating the process of turning a presumptio juris into a presumptio hominis; (b) a failure to distinguish between the inference of a presumptio hominis and the inference of a presumptio juris; (c) a failure to observe the difference between the importance of a presumptio juris in the law generally, and its importance in the particular case because of its peculiar circumstances; (d) a tendency to obey an impulse to allow a particular presumptio juris to “prevail” as a matter of law, because of its greater importance in the law generally; and (e) a failure to consider presumptions in their logical order.

All that was necessary in Castor v. Davis was to keep in proper perspective the social, moral and property aspects of the case in reaching an answer to the two presumptiones hominis mentioned above. A jury in such a case can and should be instructed along that line.

In Leach v. Hall, 95 Iowa, 611 (action to recover possession of real estate, decided in 1895), there arose questions involving the presumption of the validity of a marriage, the presumption of the continuance of a prior marriage, and the presumption of the termination of the prior marriage by a divorce. In such cases the law of Iowa sometimes allows the presumption of the divorce. “There must be something, based upon the acts and conduct of both parties, inconsistent with the continuance of the marriage relation, before the presumption should be indulged.” (Ellis v. Ellis, 58 Iowa, 720, as quoted in Leach v. Hall, at page 616.) Where it is applied, only positive proof that no divorce was obtained can be used to “rebut” it, or even to “overcome” it, and the presumption of the continuance of the prior marriage is not entertained—it is eliminated entirely from consideration. Thus the situation gives rise to two parallel presumptions, namely, that of the validity of a marriage and that of a divorce dissolving a prior marriage. Proof of the prior marriage, unaccompanied by positive proof of no divorce, is of no avail for any purpose.

Thus the question in such cases in Iowa is: Does the basis of the presumption of divorce exist?

So speaks Iowa. But she speaks illogically. What she is really telling us is, that in some cases an inference of divorce is permissible, and that that inference is one of a presumptio hominis. She does not say to the jury: “If you find so and so to be facts, you must infer there was a divorce.” She says to them: “If you find so and so to be facts, you may infer there was a divorce.” To clarify the situation, they should be told that if they infer there was no divorce the presumption of the continuance of the prior marriage must be considered, and that if they infer there was a divorce the presumption of the continuance of the prior marriage cannot be considered.

It is thus seen that the question of a divorce in Iowa, in such cases as we are now considering, is not one of a conclusive presumption, nor one of a rebuttable presumption, but one of a presumptio hominis. Upon the inference made by the presumptio hominis depends whether or not the presumption of the continuance of the prior marriage is permissible.

In each of the cases of Gibson v. Martin, 7 Humphrey (Tenn.), 127 (decided in 1846 or 1847), Weimer v. Bunbury, 30 Mich., 201 (decided in 1874), Supervisors of Houghton County v. The two presumptions absolutely and completely negatived each other, being equally broad. One of the officials or his sureties, or both, were defendants. There was a permanent presumption that he discharged his duty. The discharge of duty by the second official was brought into question collaterally. In such a situation a presumptio juris as to the discharge of his duty should not be entertained. It is not permissible, because it would or could produce a standstill or deadlock in procedure, which, as previously stated, is abhorrent to our law. As to the discharge of the duty of the second official, at most only a presumptio hominis could be permissible. A presumptio juris as to it would create a prima facie case. But the law does not recognize such a prima facie case in such cases.

In Yarnell v. The Kansas City, etc., Ry. Co., 138 Mo., 570 (decided in 1893), it was proved that plaintiff’s husband was killed by defendant’s train. It was claimed that there was a presumption that he exercised due care. The court said the railroad company was entitled to a like presumption, and added: “So that the result is that one presumption rebuts and neutralizes the other, like the conjunction of an acid with an alkali” (p. 579). The truth is, in such a case a presumptio juris that the decedent exercised due care is permissible only if the question as to the care of the decedent has been put into issue. That issue could be raised only by way of defense. If that defense is made, a presumptio juris that the decedent exercised due care would be applied—not as one in conflict with, but one in sequence with, the presumption that the defendant exercised due care. And it would be a permanent presumption, because on that issue the defendant would become actor in a “case” he would have to make out. Until such issue be made, the question of the exercise of care by the decedent could not be brought into the case at all, even collaterally, and therefore not even a presumptio hominis as to it could be permissible.

This touches upon presumptions in connection with contributory negligence. See the note in 33 L. R. A. (New Series), beginning at page 1085, which would seem to be a monument to the ability of courts—some courts—to work out problems illogically.

In negligence cases the question of care or fault on the part of the injured or killed can arise only by way of defense. A presumption that the injured or killed was guilty of negligence would be a monstrosity in the law, and opposed to the usual experience of mankind. The only alternative presumption would be that the injured or killed

<br>

### --- Page 0087 ---

observed due care. But such a presumption cannot exist and be active at the same time with the permanent presumption in favor of the defendant, because the two would be equally broad and absolutely and completely negative each other, and thus would or could produce a deadlock in procedure. The only way the question of the conduct of the injured or killed can logically be brought into question is by the defendant’s bringing it into the case. This the defendant can do only by raising an issue or defense on the point. That issue or defense becomes a “case” for the defendant to establish or make out. In it there is a presumption—a permanent presumption—that the injured or killed exercised due care. Until such issue or defense is made, the question of the conduct of the injured or killed cannot be raised in the action or suit—not even a presumptio hominis on the question is permissible—it is outside the law of proof in the case.

But what if the evidence or proof introduced by the plaintiff shows, or tends to prove, that the injured or killed was guilty of contributory negligence? Here some courts go astray. Instead of waiting for the defendant to raise the issue or defense of contributory negligence, they raise it for him—and rule upon it at the same time. That not only establishes the rule that the law will raise the issue or defense for a party, but it invades the province of the jury, for the judge undertakes to say that contributory negligence has been proved, or at least that the basis of a presumption of contributory negligence has been proved. There is no such permissible presumption in our system of law. Nor has a judge in a jury negligence case the right to say that contributory negligence has been proved.

When the issue or defense of contributory negligence is raised, a sequence of presumptions results. A permanent presumption of due care on the part of the defendant is succeeded by a permanent presumption of due care on the part of the injured or killed. There is no “conflict” of presumptions.

That is the logical situation whether the defendant makes the issue or defense of contributory negligence or the judge makes it for him.

The failure to appreciate the proper function of presumptiones juris has produced the confusion which exists in the law regarding the defense of insanity (or other mental incapacity) in criminal cases. Mental capacity is one of the essential elements of punishable crime, and must therefore be proved by the state. But expediency justifies the application of a presumptio juris of sanity (or mental capacity) in proving that element of a punishable crime. Suppose there were no recognized presumption of sanity. The state would have to produce evidence or proof to establish sanity beyond a reasonable doubt. All the defense would have to do, to secure an acquittal, would be to produce enough evidence or proof to create a reasonable doubt as to sanity. But to require such a procedure would be foolish and intolerable where it is expeditious and justly possible to introduce into practice a presumption of sanity at the outset. The law recognizes

<br>

### --- Page 0088 ---

such a presumption. The basis of it is the same as that of the presumption of innocence in a criminal case. The law thus supplies the proof of the necessary element of sanity. But as soon as “conflicting” evidence or proof on the question of sanity is introduced the presumptio juris of sanity is immediately thereby turned into a presumptio hominis under the same burden or rule of proof as if there had been no presumptio juris of sanity at all. That is to say, sanity must be proved beyond a reasonable doubt, before there can be a conviction. The defense does not have to prove insanity. It does not have to disprove sanity. It merely has to prevent proof by the prosecution of sanity beyond a reasonable doubt. There is really no “defense” of insanity in the technical sense of something that must be proved by the defendant in order to defeat conviction. All talk about the burden resting upon the accused of proving insanity, by a preponderance of the evidence or what not, is therefore illogical and fallacious. Davia v. United States, 160 U. S., 469, is interesting and instructive along that line. All that is missing in that case is a clear exposition of the process of turning a temporary presumptio juris into a presumptio hominis under the same burden or rule of proof as if there had been no such temporary presumptio juris.

So that in a criminal case involving the so-called defense of insanity there is neither a conflict nor a sequence of presumptions, as far as sanity is concerned. The permanent presumption does not conflict with the temporary presumption of sanity, because sanity is only a link in the chain of proof. Nor are the two presumptions in sequence, because the presumption of sanity does not create a prima facie case, i.e., does not affect the whole “case.” In that respect it is different from the presumption of fraud arising from a confidential relationship in a fraud case, to which we have paid some attention in the main article.

What has been said here about the presumption of sanity in a criminal case applies equally to the presumption of chastity in a criminal prosecution for seduction under a law providing that to constitute the crime the woman must have been of previously chaste character. So that cases like The State v. Wells 48 Iowa, 671, holding that in such prosecution “the defendant must not only produce such evidence as would raise a reasonable doubt of chastity, but such as would overcome the presumption of law in its favor by a fair preponderance,” do not handle the problem correctly.
